    "This method is often called the **midpoint rule**, and it's probably my \n",
    "favorite integration scheme because it's pretty accurate and dirt simple\n",
    "to implement.\n",
    "\n",
    "The alternative is to average the right- and left-sided sums, which leads\n",
    "to \n",
    "\n",
    "$$\n",
    "\\begin{split}\n",
    "  S &= \\frac{b-a}{2n} \n",
    "    \\Bigg [ \\sum^{n}_{i=1} f \\Bigg ( \\frac{ (i)(b-a) }{n} + a \\Bigg ) \n",
    "         +  \\sum^{n}_{i=1} f \\Bigg ( \\frac{ (i-1)(b-a) }{n} + a \\Bigg ) \\Bigg ] \\\\\n",
    "    &= \\frac{b-a}{2n} \n",
    "    \\Bigg [ f(a) + f(b) + \\sum^{n-1}_{i=1} f \\Bigg ( \\frac{ i(b-a) }{n} + a \\Bigg ) \\Bigg ]  \n",
    "\\end{split}\n",
    "$$\n",
    "  \n",
    "If you consider this carefully, the resulting geometric picture turns\n",
    "rectangles into trapezoids (because the average of the area of \n",
    "two rectangles of heights $y(x_1)$ and $y(x_2)$ that share \n",
    "the same $x$ coordinates $x_1$ and\n",
    "$x_2$ is equal to the area of the trapezoid whose coordinates\n",
    "include $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$),\n",
    "and the method is known as the **trapezoid rule**.  (It's those pesky\n",
    "end-point terms $(a)$ and $f(b)$\n",
    "that make this rule just a hair less convenient to \n",
    "apply in some cases, so I always stick to the midpoint rule when I can!)"

    "Finally, the integration rules just explored represent the simplest \n",
    "possible [Newton-Cotes](http://mathworld.wolfram.com/Newton-CotesFormulas.html) formulas.  The basic idea of Newton-Cotes \n",
    "integration is that a function $f(x)$ is assumed to be piece-wise\n",
    "polynomial in finite ranges and evaluated at evenly-spaced points $x_i$.  Left-sided sums, right-sided sums, and\n",
    "and the midpoint rule take \n",
    "$f(x)$ to be *constant* between adjacent points $x_i$ and\n",
    "$x_{i+1}$, while the trapezoid rule assumes $f(x)$ is \n",
    "*linear* between adjacent points.  If $f(x)$ is assumed to \n",
    "be quadratic between $x_i$ and $x_{i+2}$, the \n",
    "result is [Simpson's rule](http://mathworld.wolfram.com/SimpsonsRule.html) (another favorite of mine).  "

    "import numpy as np\n",
    "import matplotlib.pyplot as plt\n",
    "\n",
    "def right_sided_sum(f, a, b, n):\n",
    "    \"\"\"Approximate the integral of f over [a, b] using \n",
    "       the right-sided sum and n evenly spaced points.\"\"\"\n",
    "    x = np.linspace(a, b, n+1)[1:]\n",
    "    return np.sum(f(x))*(x[1]-x[0])\n",
    "\n",
    "# reference integral value\n",
    "I_ref = np.exp(1) - 1.0 \n",
    "# number of points to use in each sum\n",
    "n_vals = 2**np.arange(1, 10)\n",
    "# initialize the approximate integrals\n",
    "I_appx = np.zeros(len(n_vals))\n",
    "for i in range(len(n_vals)):\n",
    "    I_appx[i] = right_sided_sum(np.exp, 0, 1, n_vals[i])\n",
    "# absolute error\n",
    "I_err = abs(I_appx-I_ref)\n",
    "# Delta = (b-a)/(n+1)\n",
    "Delta = 1/(n_vals+1)\n",
    "# Plot the error along with linear and quadratic forms\n",
    "plt.loglog(Delta, I_err, 'k', label='error')\n",
    "plt.loglog(Delta, Delta, 'r--', label='$\\mathcal{O}(\\Delta)$')\n",
    "plt.loglog(Delta, 10*Delta**2, 'b-.', label='$\\mathcal{O}(\\Delta^2)$')\n",
    "plt.xlabel('$\\Delta$')\n",
    "plt.legend()\n",
    "plt.show()"
